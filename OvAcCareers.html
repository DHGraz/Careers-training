Academic careers

This section will give you an overview of the steps in an academic career and the different aspects of an academic job.

There are significant differences between structures, policies, and organisation of academic work in different countries. However, at its basic an academic career is always based on three separate components: research, teaching, and service. In addition, in many countries and for many funders, impact is becoming increasingly important.
Similarly, despite differences in titles or conditions, an academic career is generally based on the same steps: students can begin to work on research projects during their MA, and subsequently decide to enroll in a PhD. PhD researchers can be funded in a variety of way and their programme might have different requirments (see section on PhD), but their main aim will always be the writing of a PhD thesis. Once their thesis has been approved in a formal exam, they will become Doctor and move into the phase of postdocs. Postdocs are faced by a multiplicity of career choices: they can decide to follow a research-focused path, a teaching path, a mix of the two, or to work in research outside of the university. This tends to a be an uncertain and precarious phase for many resarchers, and in reality they might be employed on multiple contracts across all these paths. After some years, they will become established in a career track; for those pursuing a professorship, this will begin with junior professor positions and gradually continue with associate professorships and full professorships. In certain countries a junior professor can become full professor within the same university, undergoing an internal evaluation; in other countries, there might be the need to apply for positions (if and when they open), to move institutions (e.g. Germany), or to submit formal 'tenure dossiers' (e.g.US).
